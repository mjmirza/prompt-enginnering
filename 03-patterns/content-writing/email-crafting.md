# Email Crafting Pattern

Strategic templates for writing effective emails across different contexts and purposes, from professional communication to marketing outreach.

## Purpose

Create clear, purposeful, and appropriately-toned emails that achieve their intended goal while maintaining professional standards.

## When to Use

- Business correspondence
- Cold outreach
- Customer communication
- Internal team updates
- Marketing campaigns
- Apology or difficult messages
- Follow-ups and reminders
- Networking and introductions

## Template Structure

```
Write a [TYPE] email for [PURPOSE/SITUATION].

Email details:
- From: [sender role/context]
- To: [recipient role/relationship]
- Subject line: [Generate or specific]
- Primary goal: [what you want to achieve]
- Tone: [professional/friendly/formal/casual/apologetic/etc.]
- Length: [brief/moderate/detailed]

Structure:
1. Opening: [greeting and context]
2. Purpose: [main message/request/information]
3. Details: [supporting information]
4. Call-to-action: [what you want them to do]
5. Closing: [appropriate sign-off]

Constraints:
- [Any specific requirements]
- [What to avoid]
- [Key points to include]

Context:
- [Relationship background]
- [Any relevant history]
- [Cultural or organizational norms]
```

## Pattern Variations

### Professional Request Email
```
Write a professional email requesting [SPECIFIC REQUEST].

To: [Recipient name/title]
From: [Your role]
Context: [Brief background]

Key points:
- Clear reason for request
- Why you're asking them specifically
- What you need and by when
- How it helps them or mutual benefit
- Make it easy to say yes

Tone: Professional but warm
Length: Concise, 3-4 short paragraphs
```

### Cold Outreach Email
```
Create a cold outreach email to [TARGET AUDIENCE] offering [PRODUCT/SERVICE/OPPORTUNITY].

Value proposition: [Key benefit]
Differentiation: [Why different from competitors]

Structure:
- Subject: Compelling, personalized, not salesy
- Opening: Relevant to them, not about you
- Value: What's in it for them
- Proof: Brief credibility element
- CTA: Low-commitment next step
- P.S.: [Optional reinforcement]

Tone: Helpful, not pushy
Goal: Get a response, not close a sale
Length: Under 150 words
```

### Difficult/Apology Email
```
Write an email [apologizing for/addressing] [SITUATION].

Situation: [What happened]
Fault: [Who's responsible]
Impact: [Effect on recipient]

Include:
1. Acknowledgment of issue
2. Genuine apology (if appropriate)
3. Explanation (brief, not excuse)
4. What you're doing to fix it
5. How you'll prevent it
6. Next steps

Tone: Sincere, accountable, professional
Avoid: Excuses, blame-shifting, minimizing
Length: Moderate, thorough but not rambling
```

### Follow-Up Email
```
Write a follow-up email for [SITUATION/PREVIOUS INTERACTION].

Previous context: [What happened before]
Time since last contact: [Duration]
Purpose of follow-up: [Why reaching out again]

Balance:
- Persistent but not annoying
- Add value, don't just "check in"
- Give easy out if not interested
- Include new information or angle

Tone: [Professional/friendly based on relationship]
Subject: Reference previous conversation
```

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Partnership Proposal
```
Write a business partnership proposal email.

From: Marketing Director at SaaS company (500 employees)
To: Head of Partnerships at complementary service (similar size)
Context: Met briefly at conference last month, had good conversation

Proposal: Co-marketing partnership
Value for them:
- Access to our 50k user base
- Mutual customer benefit
- Shared content creation

Ask: Initial call to explore possibilities

Include:
- Reference to conference meeting
- Specific partnership idea
- Mutual benefits
- Low-pressure next step
- Brief company credibility

Tone: Professional, collaborative, enthusiastic but not pushy
Length: 3-4 paragraphs
Generate 3 subject line options
```

### Example 2: Customer Support Response
```
Write a customer support email responding to a complaint.

Situation: Customer received damaged product
Customer tone: Frustrated but reasonable
Our fault: Packaging issue, first time customer

Response should:
1. Apologize sincerely
2. Take responsibility
3. Explain what went wrong (briefly)
4. Offer solution: replacement + discount on next order
5. Timeline for resolution
6. Direct contact for questions

Tone: Empathetic, professional, solution-focused
Show: We value their business
Avoid: Corporate jargon, deflection
Urgency: Same-day response needed
```

### Example 3: Internal Team Update
```
Write a team update email about project delay.

From: Project Manager
To: Cross-functional team (8 people)
Situation: Product launch delayed 2 weeks

Include:
- Clear subject line about delay
- Brief explanation of why (vendor issue)
- New timeline
- What this means for each team
- What's still on track
- Next steps and revised deadlines
- Open for questions

Tone: Transparent, calm, solution-oriented
Style: Bullet points for clarity
Length: Scannable, under 300 words
```

### Example 4: Networking Introduction Request
```
Write an email asking for an introduction.

From: Junior professional
To: Mutual connection (former colleague)
Request: Introduction to [Name] at [Company]
Reason: Exploring career opportunities in their field

Make it easy for them:
- Why you want the intro (specific)
- Why you're qualified/interesting to that person
- Suggested introduction text they can use
- Easy to forward
- Graceful out if they're not comfortable

Tone: Respectful, appreciative, professional
Show: You value their time and relationship
```

### Example 5: Sales Follow-Up After Demo
```
Write a follow-up email after product demo.

Context:
- Demo was yesterday
- Prospect asked good questions
- Showed interest but no commitment
- Mentioned budget review in 2 weeks

Goals:
- Stay top of mind
- Provide additional value
- Move toward decision
- Schedule next step

Include:
- Thank them for time
- Recap key points they cared about
- Address any concerns raised
- Provide promised information
- Share relevant case study
- Suggest concrete next step
- Timeline awareness

Tone: Helpful consultant, not pushy salesperson
Length: 5-6 paragraphs with clear structure
```

## Best Practices

1. **Subject Line**: Clear, specific, compelling
2. **Opening**: Personalized, contextual
3. **Purpose First**: Don't bury the lead
4. **Scannability**: Short paragraphs, bullets where appropriate
5. **One Primary CTA**: Clear single action
6. **Tone Match**: Appropriate for relationship and situation
7. **Proofread**: Zero typos in professional emails
8. **Mobile-Friendly**: Most emails read on phones

## Common Pitfalls

- Vague or missing subject line
- Starting with too much small talk
- Writing too long (respect recipient's time)
- Multiple competing CTAs
- Wrong tone for situation
- Not making ask clear
- No clear next step
- Assuming too much knowledge
- Generic/impersonal
- Typos and errors

## Email Type Guide

**Request Emails**: Be clear, specific, make it easy to yes
**Apology Emails**: Own it, fix it, prevent it
**Follow-Up Emails**: Add value, don't just "check in"
**Introduction Emails**: Make it about them, not you
**Update Emails**: Bottom line up front, details below
**Thank You Emails**: Specific about what you're thanking for
**Cold Emails**: Personalized, brief, value-focused
**Rejection Emails**: Respectful, brief, leave door open

## Subject Line Formulas

**For Requests**: "[Specific ask] - [Brief context]"
**For Intros**: "[Name] intro: [Specific topic]"
**For Updates**: "Update: [Project] - [Status]"
**For Follow-ups**: "Re: [Previous topic]" or "Following up: [Topic]"
**For Cold emails**: Personalized, curiosity-driven, specific
**For Sales**: Value-focused, not feature-focused

## Tips for Optimization

- Specify exact relationship with recipient
- Mention any previous interactions
- Indicate urgency level
- Share cultural/organizational context
- Request multiple subject line options
- Specify preferred email length
- Mention if mobile optimization matters
- Indicate if formal or casual
- Request specific tone nuances

## Combining with Other Techniques

- **Email + A/B Testing**: Test subject lines/CTAs
- **Email + Personalization**: Dynamic content for scale
- **Email + Sequence**: Multi-touch campaigns
- **Email + Templates**: Reusable frameworks
- **Email + Analytics**: Track and optimize

## Advanced Variation: Email Sequence

```
Create a 3-email sequence for [PURPOSE].

Sequence goal: [Overall objective]
Target audience: [Description]
Timeline: [Email 1: Day 0, Email 2: Day X, Email 3: Day Y]

Email 1 - [FOCUS]:
- Subject: [Generate]
- Goal: [Specific goal]
- Content: [Key points]
- CTA: [Action]

Email 2 - [FOCUS]:
- Subject: [Generate]
- Trigger: [Based on email 1 response or no response]
- Goal: [Specific goal]
- Content: [Build on email 1]
- CTA: [Action]

Email 3 - [FOCUS]:
- Subject: [Generate]
- Trigger: [Condition]
- Goal: [Final push or graceful exit]
- Content: [Different angle or final value]
- CTA: [Last action or pause]

Ensure:
- Each email can stand alone
- Value increases with each touch
- Progression feels natural
- Easy unsubscribe/opt-out
```

## Tone Adjustment Examples

**Same message, different tones:**

**Formal**: "I would appreciate the opportunity to discuss this matter at your earliest convenience."

**Professional-Friendly**: "I'd love to chat about this when you have a chance."

**Casual**: "Want to hop on a quick call about this?"

**Urgent**: "Could we discuss this today? It's time-sensitive."

**Apologetic**: "I apologize for the short notice, but could we possibly discuss this soon?"
